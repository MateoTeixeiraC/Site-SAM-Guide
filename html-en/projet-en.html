The SAM-Guide Project

Our Objectives

Spatial Autonomy

The core of the SAM-Guide project is to promote the autonomy of blind or visually impaired individuals in their interactions with space. Whether it's moving within a building, locating an object, or engaging in physical activity, the loss of vision makes these tasks complex and sometimes impossible without assistance. The goal is to develop intuitive sensory tools that allow users to orient themselves, navigate, and interact with their environment without relying on human assistance or visual guidance. These tools should provide them with the freedom to move and act in a safe, fluid, and autonomous manner.

Sensory Transcoding

One of the major ambitions of SAM-Guide is to design a universal sensory language capable of converting spatial information — usually transmitted by vision — into tactile and auditory signals. This process, called sensory transcoding, is based on principles from neuroscience, spatial cognition, and ergonomics. It aims to transmit only the essential information in an intelligible and intuitive manner while respecting the cognitive limits of the user. The goal is to create a coherent and modular system that can be used in different situations and adapted to various interfaces (belt, headset, etc.).

Inclusive Technologies

SAM-Guide aims to design technologies that are truly accessible and tailored to the specific needs of blind or visually impaired individuals. The project prioritizes an inclusive approach: the developed devices are lightweight, portable, compatible with urban mobility or sports activities, and do not require complex training. The idea is to move away from a total substitution of vision and instead offer concrete, simple tools that can be easily integrated into daily life. By improving their accessibility in various contexts, these technologies contribute to a fairer and more inclusive society.

Technologies Used

Tactile Belt

The vibrating belt developed as part of SAM-Guide is a device worn around the waist, equipped with several vibrating motors positioned all around the body. Each vibration is used to transmit directional information: for example, a vibration on the right indicates a direction to the right. This device allows users to anticipate a turn or align themselves on a path, even in the absence of a visual reference. The use of the belt limits the hands and attention required, making it particularly useful in situations where safety is paramount (crossing streets, sports activities, etc.).

Spatialized Sound

Spatialized sound is another key component of the project. Using audio processing algorithms based on HRTF (Head-Related Transfer Function), the sounds generated by the device can be perceived as coming from a specific point in the space around the user. This allows, for example, to locate an object or an obstacle by sound. This device significantly improves orientation and perception of the environment. It also requires spatial precision, especially in complex or dynamic environments such as outdoor movements.

Capture Systems and Augmented Reality

To design and test technologies, the project uses enriched digital environments that are adjusted in real-time using motion capture systems. This allows for the simulation of complex spaces in which users can be guided and oriented through sensory signals. Augmented reality allows for precise modeling of the environment, detection of interactions, and adaptation of signals to the user's movements and attention, in order to optimize usability and personalization.

Project Progress

2021
Launch of the SAM-Guide project, funded by ANR, for a duration of 48 months.
Creation of a multidisciplinary consortium, bringing together researchers in neuroscience, engineering, computer science, and ergonomics.
DDefinition of scientific objectives, focused on sensory transcoding and spatial interaction without vision.
2022
First face-to-face exchanges, with the kick-off meeting at École Polytechnique.
Theoretical modeling of the 3D beacon system, the basis of the spatial language developed in the project.
Preparation of experimental platforms in augmented reality and motion capture.
2023
Development of the first prototypes of the tactile belt and spatialized sounds.
First user tests in AR environments, to evaluate perception and navigation.
Strategic recruitments (PhD students and engineers) to strengthen the teams in Rouen, Caen, and Grenoble.
2024
Validation of combined scenarios: navigation, object localization, dynamic interaction.
Integration of new algorithms for line detection, obstacle avoidance, and data fusion.
Experimentation on concrete cases, notably through the adapted Laser-Run activity.
2025
Finalization of devices and protocols for use in real conditions.
Dissemination and scientific valorization through publications and open source.
Exploration of new applications in mobility, sports, and urban accessibility.

Practical Applications

Laser-Run

The adapted Laser-Run is a concrete application of the SAM-Guide project, designed as a playful and sensory testing ground for blind or visually impaired individuals.

Mobility Training

SAM-Guide devices can be used in training or rehabilitation pathways to assist in learning autonomous navigation both indoors and outdoors.

Assisted Urban Navigation

These technologies can be adapted to guidance systems in cities or complex locations, to enhance daily autonomy.
