An ANR project to assist people with visual impairments through the development of multimodal systems

Our research project

Objectives

SAM-Guide aims to improve the quality of life of people with visual impairments by providing them with technological tools that allow them to interact with their environment autonomously. The project is based on three fundamental axes: navigation without human assistance, reaching objects in nearby or distant space, and participating in physical activities such as laser-run. Each of these dimensions addresses specific needs expressed by users, complementing existing mobility training.

Multimodality

By combining spatialized sound (binaural) and vibrating tactile feedback, SAM-Guide offers a multimodal approach to the transmission of spatial information. This combination helps to overcome the limitations of each modality taken in isolation and provides beneficial sensory redundancy for learning. Through intuitive, directional, and dynamic signals, the user can better orient themselves in space, understand the distance to an object or the direction to follow, and adapt their movements in real-time. This design principle enhances user comfort and limits cognitive overload.

Interdisciplinarity

The project relies on close collaboration between several disciplines: cognitive neuroscience, embedded computing, applied mathematics, ergonomic psychology, and systems engineering. Brought together in a national consortium (Université Grenoble Alpes, ENS Paris-Saclay, Université de Rouen, and Université de Caen), these areas of expertise enable a comprehensive approach to the technical and human challenges. Interdisciplinarity ensures more robust, scalable, and user-centered solutions, relying on user testing, immersive scenarios, and scientifically validated technologies.

The SAM-Guide project brings together several laboratories across France, each with a specific mission: sound spatialization in Paris, guidance in Rouen, interaction in Grenoble, design in Caen, etc. These complementary areas of expertise work hand in hand to build an accessible system, tested in the field and designed for real-world use.
